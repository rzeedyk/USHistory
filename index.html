The Declaration of Independence

It's the summer of 1776 in Philadelphia. The Continental Congress is meeting, and there's more drama than a season finale of "Stranger Things." You're part of the committee tasked with drafting the Declaration of Independence.

Thomas Jefferson is hunched over his desk, quill in hand, looking like he's about to drop the hottest track of 1776. But he's stuck on the opening lines.

Jefferson turns to you and asks, "How should we start this revolutionary document?"

Life, Liberty, and the Pursuit of Happiness

Jefferson's eyes light up like he just got the latest iPhone (if iPhones existed in 1776). "That's perfect!" he exclaims.

As the committee works on the document, John Adams bursts in. "The British are sending more troops!" he shouts. "We need to decide: do we ask for full independence now, or try one last time for peace?"

We the People...

Jefferson scratches his head, looking more confused than a cat watching a magic trick. "That sounds more like the opening for a constitution," he says. "But let's keep it in mind for later!"

Suddenly, Benjamin Franklin rushes in, his kite and key forgotten in the excitement. "I've got it!" he shouts. "What if we start with 'When in the course of human events...'"

The room buzzes with excitement. What do you think?
